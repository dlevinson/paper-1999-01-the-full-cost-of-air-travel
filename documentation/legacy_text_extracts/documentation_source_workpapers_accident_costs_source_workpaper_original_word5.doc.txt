1. Introduction
	Accidents afflict all modes of transportation, but rates for highway accidents are much higher than for air or rail.  Over 40,000 people die in the United States every year in highway accidents, over 5 million are injured (Miller, 1991).  Moreover, accidents cause countless hours of delay to those who are stuck in traffic behind them.    This section will provide estimates on the cost of accidents for highway and air transportation modes.   It has been found after many years of operation in both Japan and France that high speed rail can be made safe from accidents.  So high speed rail is not discussed here, its cost of accidents beyond its capital and operating costs can be effectively considered zero.
	The next section will discuss accident rates for highway and air transportation.  This will be followed by a discussion of the damage costs of accidents.  The idea of protection is introduced, but no data is available to establish an accident prevention supply curve.  Finally, the accident rates are combined with damage costs to estimate the full cost of life and safety from accidents for highway and air travel. Cost allocation, incidence, and recovery are discussed.  The inter-relationship between accident costs and costs from other sectors is touched upon.

3. Damage Costs of Accidents
3.1 Methods of Measurement
	Years Lost plus Direct Cost - This method estimates the number of years lost to an accident due to death and years lost from non-fatal injuries.  It also estimates the monetary costs of non-life damages.  However it keeps life in non-monetary terms.   While it may have some moral advantages in that we need place a dollar value on life, there are very practical reasons for doing so - how do we assess whether an improvement, with a certain construction cost and life-saving potential, is worthwhile?
	Comprehensive -  The comprehensive method extends the Years Lost plus Direct Cost method by placing a value on human life.  The principle means for doing so is by looking at the trade-offs people make when choosing to conduct an activity with a certain risk level versus another activity at a different risk, but different cost/time.   Studies are based both on what people actually pay and what they are willing to pay, and use a variety of revealed preference techniques.  This method is preferred by the US Federal Highway Administration
	Human Capital - This method is used in the Australian study Social Cost of Road Accidents.  However Miller (1991) and others dislike the method because the only effects of injury that it counts is out-of-pocket costs plus lost work and housework.  By extension, it places low value on children and perhaps even a negative value on the elderly.  While measuring human capital is a necessary input to the costs of accidents, it cannot be the only input.
	Willingness-to-Pay -Jones-Lee (1990) discusses the willingness-to-pay approach to valuing safety.   Willingness-to-pay studies generally fall into two classes: revealed preference and stated preference.  Each apprach has problems, for instance revealed preference studies which compare wage and risks may pick up what regulators  are willing to allow rather than what the market price would be.   Stated preference has the problem that without real consequences or complete information, accurate estimates of value are difficult to make.

4. Protection Costs of Accidents

	Are accidents fungible?  If we expect that a facility will result in X accidents, is it sufficient to say that we can compensate for its damages by reducing the total number of expected accidents  by X throughout the transportation system? Or must we have 0 accidents on the facility in question in order to say the damages are irrelevant?

	It is the argument of this paper that accidents are fungible, and that protection measures undertaken (on or off the facility) can offset the increase in accidents due to a new project.  However, the scope of this report did not permit us to establish the marginal cost per life saved of a safety improvement.

	Ideally, the methodology would be derived from a large sample of engineering studies, ranked based on cost effectiveness, to approximate a supply curve of accident protection.  Zegeer (1990) has examined this issue to a small extent on horizontal curves, but to date there is no comprehensive study.

6. Incidence, Cost Allocation, and Recovery
	Obviously, some costs of crashes fall on involved individuals, some on their insurance companies, and some on society at large.  Therefore some of the costs are internal to the driver, some are external to the driver, but internal to transportation, and some are external to transportation.
	The externality effects of transport risk can be divided into two categories: the risk to the driver himself, and the risk to everyone else (other drivers, passenger, pedestrians, etc.).  These include death, injury and disablement, and property damage.  The paper provides estimates of these costs.  It then discusses means for internalizing these costs.
	Transport safety in the common carrier segment has significant asymmetries of information.  For instance, the bus company knows the condition of the vehicle much better than the passenger.  This is an argument for regulation.  There is also the consequence of a disaster, associated with a common carrier, which garners media attention, unlike a single car accident.

INTER-RELATIONSHIPS
The relationship of safety costs to  other cost sectors must be recognized.  First are infrastructure.   Clearly some part of infrastructure cost is based on design standards to ensure a certain safety level.  It is too much to claim that high speed rail has no safety costs, though there are no recorded accidents.  Rather,  a high investment in safety, mostly compatible with its investment in speed, has been undertaken to avoid accidents up front through system design.

	Similarly, carrier or vehicle costs are based in part on regulations and consumer choice to ensure certain safety level.  The reason the auto fatality rates have declined is in part due to higher standards.  Vehicle costs have risen, while accident costs have declined.  We hope the trade-off was beneficial.


	Accidents are clearly one of the primary causes of non-recurring congestion, to which class (accident, congestion) do we attribute the cost?  There also should be some consistency between the value of life and the value of time, as Ezra Hauer (1993) put it ‚ÄúIs it better to be dead than stuck in traffic?‚Äù

	The relationships between accidents and the other sectors are more fluid, for instance the cost of the rare hazardous materials accident, the value of non-recurring noise, and temporary social severance due to an accident.
